Why these tiers?

How dopaFit classifies activity, and what counts as healthy.

The three-tier ladder

High spike
Slot-machine mechanics. Designed to be hard to stop.

Variable reward. Infinite scroll. Parasocial hooks. The loop runs whether you're aware or not.

What's in it: Twitter/X, Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, Threads, Snapchat, doomscroll news (HN, CNN, BBC, Daily Mail), Tinder/Bumble/Hinge, betting (DraftKings, Bet365), YouTube Shorts URLs.

Healthy budget: < 30 min/day. Threshold matches Hunt et al. 2018 (n=143) — limiting social media to 30 min/day reduced loneliness and depression in a 3-week RCT.
Medium spike
Semi-intentional. Real value, but easy to overshoot.

You opened it for a reason. The question is whether you closed it for a reason too. Session length is the failure mode here, not the activity itself.

What's in it: YouTube, Netflix, Twitch, Spotify, Reddit, Quora, Substack, Discord, Slack, WhatsApp Web, shopping (Amazon, Etsy, AliExpress), AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Copilot, v0, Lovable), finance scrolling (TradingView, Yahoo Finance), LinkedIn.

Healthy budget: < 90 min/day total, with no single session over 45 min. AI sub-threshold: ~27 min/day (MIT/OpenAI RCT, n=981).
Positive (high-leverage)
Effortful. Build, work, study, research. Positive dopamine that compounds.

Output exceeds consumption. Requires deliberate attention. Doesn't hijack reward systems.

What's in it: Code editors and dev sites (GitHub, GitLab, StackOverflow, MDN, npm, Vercel, Supabase), work tools (Notion, Linear, Figma, Google Docs, Calendar, Mail), learning (Coursera, Khan Academy, Brilliant, LeetCode, freeCodeCamp), research (Wikipedia, Scholar, arXiv).

Healthy budget: No cap. Break every ~90 min (ultradian cycles, Kleitman BRAC). Sustained focus past 90 min without a break degrades — even on productive work.

How a domain lands in a tier

I classify a site by whichever set of criteria it best satisfies. When it's borderline, I bias toward the more conservative tier.

High

  • Variable, unpredictable reward
  • Infinite feed / autoplay
  • Engineered for hard-to-stop
  • Parasocial / social-validation hooks

Medium

  • Genuine utility for some uses
  • Easy to keep going past intent
  • Session length is the failure mode
  • Includes AI chat — see below

Positive

  • Requires effort to use
  • Output > consumption ratio
  • No reward-system hijack
  • Build, work, study, research

Why AI tools are in medium, not low

The most-contested classification.

The intuition says: "Claude is a productivity tool, like GitHub." The research disagrees:

  • MIT/OpenAI RCT, n=981, 4 weeks (2025): Above ~27 min/day of ChatGPT use correlates with more loneliness, more emotional dependence, and less real-world socialization. Effect is dose-dependent.
  • METR study, 2025: Developers think AI makes them 20% faster. Measured: 19% slower. A 40-point perception gap. This is the vibe-coding tell.
  • GitClear 2025 (211M lines, 2020–2024): Code churn up, refactoring down, code clones up 8× in 2024. Output quality drops even when output speed feels great.
  • Microsoft / CMU survey, 2025: Higher AI confidence ↔ less critical-thinking effort. Authors literally use the word atrophied.
  • CHI 2025 — "Dark Addiction Patterns of AI Chatbot Interfaces": Maps slot-machine mechanics (variable reward, near-miss, sycophancy as social reward) onto LLM UIs.

Verdict. AI is not social media — there's no scroll loop. But AI is not pure productivity either — there's real cognitive offloading and dose-dependent dependence. Medium tier with a 45-min session-streak alert is the honest middle. The streak alert mirrors the literature: sub-45-min sessions are typically task-mode; beyond that is where the perception gap and dependence patterns kick in.

Healthy benchmark per person (adults)

Numbers below are recreational screen time — on top of whatever work-screen time your job requires. Productive (low tier) does not count toward the recreational total.

Category Daily target Source
High tier (social, doomscroll, betting) < 30 min Hunt et al. 2018, J. Soc. Clin. Psychology
